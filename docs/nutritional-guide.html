Nutritional Guide

A summary of what peer-reviewed nutrition science says about balanced eating and fasting — grounded in the strongest available human evidence, with honest notes on uncertainty. This is a reference for the principles behind the food choices on this site, not a prescription.

What "Balanced" Actually Means

The phrase "balanced diet" is vague in popular use. Scientifically it means meeting all essential macro- and micronutrient needs without chronic excess of anything that causes measurable harm. The most rigorous attempt to define this at a global level is the EAT–Lancet Commission report (Willett et al., Lancet, 2019), which synthesized evidence across health outcomes and environmental sustainability. Its core recommendations:

  • Predominantly plant-based foods: vegetables, fruits, whole grains, legumes, and nuts
  • Modest amounts of dairy and eggs; seafood and poultry as optional additions
  • Minimal red and processed meat, added sugars, and refined starches
  • Unsaturated fats (olive oil, nuts, seeds) over saturated fats

This pattern is consistent across most long-term epidemiological evidence on diet and mortality.

Dietary Patterns with Strong Evidence

Mediterranean Diet Well established

The most extensively studied dietary pattern in controlled human trials. The PREDIMED trial (Estruch et al., NEJM, 2018; n=7,447) found that a Mediterranean diet supplemented with extra-virgin olive oil or nuts reduced major cardiovascular events by ~30% over five years compared to a low-fat control diet. A meta-analysis by Sofi et al. (BMJ, 2008; 12 studies, 1.5M person-years) found that each 2-point increase in Mediterranean diet adherence was associated with a 9% reduction in all-cause mortality.

Core features: abundant vegetables, legumes, whole grains, and olive oil; herbs and spices over salt; moderate dairy and eggs; low red meat and ultra-processed food. The pattern matters more than any individual food.

Whole-Food Plant-Based Diet Well established

A large prospective cohort study (Song et al., JAMA Internal Medicine, 2016; n=131,342) found that replacing 3% of calories from animal protein with plant protein was associated with a 10% lower risk of all-cause mortality, with the effect strongest when replacing processed red meat. The effect was dose-dependent and consistent across subgroups.

Legumes in particular show unusually strong epidemiological support. A cross-cultural longitudinal study across five countries found legume consumption was the single strongest dietary predictor of survival in older adults, independent of ethnicity, smoking, BMI, and income. (Darmadi-Blackberry et al., Asia Pacific Journal of Clinical Nutrition, 2004; PMID 15228991)

Macronutrients

Protein

The standard adult RDA of 0.8g/kg/day is a minimum to prevent deficiency, not a target for health or performance. For people doing regular endurance or resistance exercise, the evidence supports higher intake. A systematic review and meta-analysis (Morton et al., British Journal of Sports Medicine, 2018; 49 studies, n=1,863) found protein intake beyond ~1.62g/kg/day produced no additional gains in muscle mass from resistance training. For endurance athletes, 1.2–1.6g/kg/day is a commonly cited evidence-based range to support muscle repair and glycogen replenishment.

In a vegetarian context, no single plant protein provides all essential amino acids in ideal ratios. Combining legumes with grains over the course of a day (not necessarily at the same meal) provides complete coverage. Tofu, edamame, and dairy are exceptions — complete proteins on their own.

Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates are the primary fuel for aerobic exercise above roughly 65% VO₂max. Muscle and liver glycogen is the rate-limiting fuel for sustained moderate-to-hard efforts. The evidence for adequate carbohydrate intake supporting endurance performance is robust; evidence for low-carb diets benefiting high-intensity endurance athletes is limited and inconsistent.

For cycling commuters: carbohydrates before and after the ride are not optional — they determine glycogen availability for the next effort. Beans, rice, sweet potato, and whole grains are all high-quality sources that also provide fiber, micronutrients, and protein.

Dietary Fats

Fat quality matters more than fat quantity. The evidence strongly supports replacing saturated fats with unsaturated fats, particularly polyunsaturated fats. For omega-3 fatty acids, EPA and DHA (found in fatty fish and algae) have the most consistent cardiovascular evidence. ALA — found in flaxseed, chia, and walnuts — is the plant omega-3, but conversion to EPA and DHA is poor in most adults: typically ~5–15% of ALA converts to EPA and ~0.5–5% to DHA. (Burdge & Calder, Reproduction Nutrition Development, 2005; doi:10.1051/rnd:2005047)

This means vegetarians who eat substantial flaxseed and chia are likely not meeting EPA/DHA needs from those sources alone. Algae-derived omega-3 supplements (the source fish themselves use) address this directly and are the most evidence-aligned option for vegetarians.

Fiber and the Gut Microbiome

Dietary fiber is one of the most consistently protective dietary components across epidemiological literature, associated with lower rates of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, and colorectal cancer. Much of this effect is mediated by gut bacteria that ferment fiber into short-chain fatty acids — particularly butyrate — which regulate intestinal barrier function, systemic inflammation, and metabolic signaling. A landmark review by Sonnenburg & Bäckhed (Nature, 2016) synthesized how diet shapes the microbiome and how the microbiome in turn shapes host metabolism.

A randomized controlled trial from the Sonnenburg lab (Wastyk et al., Cell, 2021; n=36, 17-week intervention) directly compared high-fiber and high-fermented-food diets. The fermented food diet — kefir, yogurt, kimchi, kombucha, fermented vegetables — produced a larger increase in microbiome diversity and a broader reduction in 19 inflammatory proteins than the high-fiber diet alone. The two diets had largely non-overlapping effects, meaning both have independent value.

Practical implication: diversity of plant foods matters as much as total fiber quantity. Thirty or more distinct plant foods per week is a useful target (herbs and spices count). Fermented foods add a distinct benefit that fiber alone does not replicate.

Intermittent Fasting: What the Evidence Shows

Intermittent fasting (IF) refers to eating patterns that cycle between defined periods of eating and fasting. The main human-studied protocols:

  • Time-restricted eating (TRE) — all eating within a 6–10 hour daily window (e.g., 16:8 means 16 hours fasting, 8-hour eating window)
  • Alternate-day fasting (ADF) — alternating very low calorie days (~500 kcal) with unrestricted days
  • 5:2 diet — two non-consecutive 500–600 kcal days per week; five unrestricted days
  • Prolonged fasting / fasting-mimicking diet — 2–5 day multi-day fasts; not recommended for active individuals in most contexts

The most comprehensive review of IF evidence is de Cabo & Mattson (NEJM, 2019), which found consistent evidence across animal models and human trials for improvements in insulin sensitivity, blood glucose, blood pressure, resting heart rate, and lipid profiles. Mechanistically, fasting shifts fuel metabolism from glucose toward fat oxidation and triggers autophagy — a cellular self-repair process that degrades and recycles damaged proteins and organelles.

Time-Restricted Eating Well established (metabolic markers) Emerging (long-term outcomes)

A rigorous RCT by Sutton et al. (Cell Metabolism, 2018; n=8, crossover) showed that early TRE — eating from 8am to 2pm, 18-hour fast — improved insulin sensitivity, blood pressure, and oxidative stress markers in men with prediabetes without caloric restriction and without weight loss. The metabolic benefit appears partly driven by alignment with circadian biology: metabolic processes are measurably more efficient in the morning, and eating late in the day blunts insulin response even when total calories are matched.

Alternate-Day Fasting Emerging

A 12-month RCT by Trepanowski et al. (JAMA Internal Medicine, 2017; n=100) compared ADF to daily caloric restriction and a control diet in metabolically healthy obese adults. Both ADF and daily restriction produced similar weight loss (~6%) and similar improvements in cardiovascular risk markers at 6 and 12 months. ADF offered no additional benefit over daily restriction, but it was a feasible alternative for people who find daily restriction difficult to sustain — adherence was comparable between groups.

Fasting Considerations for Active and Vegetarian Individuals

Glycogen, Performance, and Fasting Compatibility

Fasted endurance exercise depletes glycogen faster and increases reliance on fat oxidation. This can improve fat-burning capacity over time but impairs high-intensity performance and can compromise training quality. The weight of evidence for regular moderate-to-high intensity exercise — including cycle commuting — supports ensuring adequate carbohydrate availability before and after exercise, which is easier to manage within a 10–12 hour eating window than a strict 16:8 protocol.

Practical note for cyclists: If using TRE, structure your eating window around your ride — not the other way around. Riding fasted in the morning is feasible for easy efforts but may impair harder sessions. Post-ride glycogen replenishment within 30–60 minutes supports recovery more effectively than extending the fast.

TRE and Training Adaptations Emerging

A randomized trial by Moro et al. (Journal of Translational Medicine, 2016; n=34, 8 weeks) found that a 16:8 TRE protocol in resistance-trained men produced similar muscle retention and strength gains to a normal eating schedule, with greater reductions in fat mass and some inflammatory markers. This suggests TRE is compatible with muscle preservation in resistance-trained athletes. Generalizability to endurance athletes and women is uncertain.

Women and Fasting Use caution

Most IF trials have enrolled predominantly overweight or obese adults, often skewing male. There is emerging evidence that aggressive fasting may have different hormonal effects in lean, highly active women. Low energy availability in female athletes can disrupt hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal axis signaling, leading to menstrual dysfunction, bone density loss, and impaired performance — a constellation formally defined as Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S). A consensus statement from the IOC (Mountjoy et al., British Journal of Sports Medicine, 2018) defines diagnostic criteria and management. Women with low body weight, high training load, or any history of menstrual irregularity should be cautious about strict fasting protocols.

Iron Absorption and Meal Timing

Fasting windows can be structured to improve iron absorption — a meaningful consideration for vegetarians with low iron history. Non-heme iron (the form in plant foods) is enhanced by vitamin C consumed at the same meal and inhibited by tannins (tea, coffee, matcha), calcium (dairy), and phytates (whole grains in large quantities). If using any fasting structure, placing your highest-iron meals away from tea and dairy-heavy meals is the highest-leverage change. This does not require a strict IF protocol — deliberate meal composition is sufficient.

What the Evidence Actually Supports

Diet quality comes first. The evidence for dietary pattern quality — plenty of vegetables, legumes, whole grains, and fermented foods; minimal ultra-processed food — is stronger and more consistent than evidence for any specific meal timing strategy.

A 10–12 hour eating window is reasonable and probably beneficial. Finishing dinner by 7–8pm and eating breakfast by 7–9am aligns with circadian biology and has demonstrated metabolic benefit without requiring caloric restriction. This is the minimum-friction version of TRE.

Earlier eating windows outperform later ones. All else equal, eating between 8am and 6pm appears more metabolically beneficial than 12pm to 8pm, based on circadian physiology. But any structured window is likely better than completely unstructured eating.

Aggressive fasting is not necessary and may be counterproductive for active people. 24-hour fasts and very-low-calorie days may impair training quality, recovery, and — particularly in lean active women — hormonal function. Overnight fasts of 12–14 hours are sufficient to trigger autophagy and metabolic benefits without compromising performance.

Protein timing matters. Spreading protein across meals rather than loading it all into one sitting supports muscle protein synthesis more effectively throughout the day. A practical target: 25–35g protein per main meal.
