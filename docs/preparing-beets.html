How to Prepare Beets

Beets are earthy, naturally sweet, and packed with folate, potassium, and dietary nitrates. How you cook them changes both flavor and nutrient retention significantly. This page covers every major method with protocol and tradeoffs.

General prep for all methods: Trim the greens to about 1 inch above the root (leaving a stub prevents bleeding). Scrub well. Leave the skin on during cooking — it peels off easily afterward and prevents color and nutrient loss.

Cooking Methods

Boiled

The most common method. Quick, hands-off, but loses the most water-soluble nutrients.

  1. Place whole, unpeeled beets in a pot. Cover with cold water by 2 inches.
  2. Bring to a boil, then reduce to a steady simmer.
  3. Cook 30–45 minutes for medium beets, 20–25 for small, up to 60 for large. A knife should slide in with no resistance.
  4. Drain and cool in cold water for 5 minutes.
  5. Rub skins off under cold running water using your hands or a paper towel.

Steamed

The best cooked method for nutrient retention. Nutrients stay in the beet rather than leaching into the water.

  1. Set a steamer basket over 1–2 inches of boiling water in a lidded pot.
  2. Add whole, unpeeled beets. Cover tightly.
  3. Steam 35–50 minutes for medium beets, topping up water as needed.
  4. Test doneness with a knife — same as boiling.
  5. Cool briefly and peel under cold water.

Oven Roasted

Concentrates sweetness and flavor as moisture evaporates. Hands-off once in the oven.

  1. Preheat oven to 400°F (200°C).
  2. Rub each beet lightly with olive oil and a pinch of salt.
  3. Wrap individually in foil, or place in a covered baking dish with a splash of water.
  4. Roast 45–75 minutes depending on size. Unwrap and test with a knife.
  5. Let cool in the foil for 10 minutes, then slip skins off by hand.
For a caramelized exterior, unwrap for the last 15 minutes to let the surface dry and brown.

Grilled

Adds char and smokiness. Works best with a parboil first to ensure even cooking.

  1. Parboil whole beets for 15–20 minutes until just tender but not fully cooked. Cool and peel.
  2. Slice into ½-inch rounds or wedges. Toss with olive oil, salt, and pepper.
  3. Grill over medium-high heat 4–5 minutes per side until grill marks form and edges char slightly.
  4. Alternatively, skip parboiling and grill raw slices over medium heat for 8–10 minutes per side (more char, slightly firmer).

Pressure Cooked (Instant Pot)

Fastest hands-off method. Nutrient retention is better than boiling because cooking time is much shorter.

  1. Add 1 cup of water to the Instant Pot. Place whole, unpeeled beets on the trivet.
  2. Seal and cook on High Pressure: 15 minutes for small, 20 for medium, 25 for large.
  3. Quick-release pressure. Test with a knife — if not done, reseal and add 5 more minutes.
  4. Cool in cold water and peel.

Raw

Maximum nutrient retention. Works well grated into salads, sliced thin on a mandoline, or spiralized.

  1. Scrub and peel the raw beet with a vegetable peeler.
  2. Grate coarsely for slaws and grain bowls; slice paper-thin on a mandoline for salads; spiralize for noodle-style dishes.
  3. Dress immediately with acid (lemon, vinegar) to preserve color and add brightness.
  4. Raw beet is firmer and earthier than cooked — a 10-minute soak in salted water mellows the flavor slightly.

Beets are one of the best dietary sources of inorganic nitrates, which the body converts to nitric oxide — supporting blood pressure and exercise performance. Most nitrates survive moderate cooking; boiling loses the most as they leach into the water. Save boiling water to use as a broth base or discard.
Don't throw out the greens. Beet greens are edible and nutritious — higher in iron and calcium than the root. Sauté with garlic and olive oil like you would Swiss chard.
